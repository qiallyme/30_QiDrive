The strain of managing everything alone has become overwhelming. My husband's absence has left a void that feels impossible to fill, and the situation has only worsened as he has essentially abandoned me, offering no help or support during this crisis. The weight of running the household and businesses single-handedly continues to crush my spirit.

Adding to this immense burden, my mother is now in critical condition in the hospital's burn unit following an explosion. This devastating development has left me feeling completely lost and helpless. As her primary caregiver and only support system, I am torn between managing the crumbling remains of our business ventures and being there for her during this traumatic time.

The emotional toll of these compounding challenges has intensified my struggles with depression and anxiety. My appetite continues to diminish, and I find myself turning to unhealthy coping mechanisms just to make it through each day. The isolation feels suffocating as I attempt to navigate these overwhelming circumstances without the support system I desperately need.

---

I reached out to an ex-partner who works in a hospital, thinking he might be at the one where my mom was admitted. When I took a moment to respond, he bombarded me with angry messages about not answering—the last thing I needed in that moment.

I blocked him and sent this email.

Hi Ron,

I’ve taken some time to reflect on our exchange, and I need to be honest with you—not to blame, but to create clarity.

When I reached out, I was in a very fragile state. I had just received a voicemail about my mom possibly being in the hospital, and I was overwhelmed. I told you I was scared, paralyzed, and “hanging on by a thread.” I wasn’t ignoring you—I was frozen and trying to survive the moment.

Your initial willingness to help meant something. But what followed hit me hard.

You said things like:

“You need to get it together.”

“You let yourself go.”

“You potty yourself so much when you should be lucky.”

“I don’t recognize you.”

“Answer the damn call dammit.”

I know those words may have come from frustration or concern, but they didn’t land as love. They landed as pressure, judgment, and shame—at a time I already felt like I was breaking.

You know that phone anxiety is one of my triggers. You know I’ve gone through a lot. So being spoken to like that while I was barely holding it together was painful, not motivating.

Right now, I need to protect my peace and my energy. That’s why I’ve chosen to block communication for the time being—not to cut you off forever, but to give myself the space I need to recover, ground, and process.

This doesn’t mean I don’t care. It means I’m caring for myself first.

I hope one day we can speak again with more understanding and gentleness on both sides. But for now, I need stillness.

- Cody
