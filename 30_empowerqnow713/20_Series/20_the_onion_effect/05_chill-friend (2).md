# The Onion Effect – Episode 5
## Being the "Chill Friend": The Emotional Labor You're Not Supposed to Talk About

You ever realize you're the one who always goes with the flow?

Like, everyone else gets to be picky, tired, late, moody, dramatic—even selfish.

But you? You're the "easy one." The chill friend. The one who doesn't make a fuss.

You laugh it off when plans change.

You say "no worries" when someone cancels last-minute.

You eat the side dish you didn't order. You ride in the back seat. You take the couch.

You don't speak up about the thing that upset you because it's not worth ruining the vibe.

That's not just flexibility. That's emotional labor.

And when it's not reciprocated, it turns into resentment.

This is The Onion Effect in friendship form.

Not because your friends are bad people—but because you've been taught to prioritize their comfort over your own. You've played the role so well, they don't even know you're acting.

But what they see as "low-maintenance," you feel as invisible.

Because when you finally do need something—real support, real care, a break from being "the strong one"—you don't know how to ask. You've trained everyone not to worry about you.

So they don't.

You start to wonder if they love you, or just the version of you that never takes up space.

And that's the rub:

People will mirror what you model. If you model self-sacrifice, they'll expect it.

If you hide your boundaries, they'll treat you like you don't have any.

And slowly, the onion effect sets in—layer after layer of unspoken hurts, quiet letdowns, and swallowed needs.

But here's the truth:

You're allowed to need things.

You're allowed to be inconvenient.

You're allowed to take up space.

Even with your friends.

Especially with your friends.

And what would it look like if your friendships made room for your full self, not just your flexibility?
