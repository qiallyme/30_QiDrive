# The Onion Effect – Episode 1
## No Onions, Please: How a Burger Order Becomes a Social Filter

Every time I order food, there's a moment of negotiation.

Not about price or portion size—about onions. Mustard. Pickles. The toppings that seem harmless to most but completely derail a meal for me.

"Can I get a double cheeseburger, plain—just meat, cheese, and bun?"

I've said that line more times than I can count. At drive-thrus, diners, hotel kiosks, food trucks. And almost every time, I'm met with some variation of a look: confusion, judgment, disbelief, or that little laugh that says, "You're one of those."

But I'm not picky. I'm tired.

Tired of the internal calculation I make every time I want a bite of something comforting but know I'll have to explain, defend, or redo it. Tired of strategizing where to eat based on who will make something without making me feel weird for asking.

And it's not just fast food. It's potlucks. It's dinner invites. It's birthday parties where I quietly don't eat because I don't want to be the "difficult" one. And even when people try to accommodate me, I still feel the weight of having to say, "Actually… can we make mine separate?"

That's not just a dietary preference—it's social labor.

It's being reminded, every single time, that my body and brain don't align with the default settings of the group. That I am other. And that choosing to protect my own comfort might come at the cost of group harmony.

And yeah, maybe one burger doesn't matter. But I eat out five times a week. That's hundreds of minor confrontations a year, thousands over a lifetime. That's real wear and tear on my nervous system.

It's not about food.

It's about how I've had to build a whole system around not being misunderstood.

I skip events where food is the focus. I pre-plan exit strategies. I try to get the cook on the phone in advance, just to avoid the awkwardness. All because I've learned that my preferences are seen as "extra." When the truth is—they're bare minimum for me to feel safe, welcome, and able to participate.

So when I say The Onion Effect is real, this is what I mean.

It's one thing to order a plain burger.

It's another to carry the burden of that choice across your whole life—socially, emotionally, energetically—without anyone seeing the weight you're carrying.

---

**What's Your Onion?**

What's the thing you're always dodging?

Maybe it's not food. Maybe it's being misgendered, being touched when you don't like it, being called a nickname you hate, or constantly having to explain your "weird" hobby. Maybe it's having to act okay in group settings when you're falling apart inside. Whatever it is—if it's small, persistent, and quietly exhausting… that's your onion.

Drop it in the comments. Let it out. No disclaimers, no justifications. Just truth.

Because once we name the onion, we can stop pretending it doesn't exist.
