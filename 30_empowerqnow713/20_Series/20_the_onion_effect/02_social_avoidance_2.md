# The Onion Effect – Episode 2
## I Didn't Go Because I Knew There'd Be Onions: Social Avoidance and the Quiet Opt-Out

Sometimes I don't say no out loud.

I just… don't go.

I'll decline the invite, say I'm tired, say I've got something else to do. But really? I'm avoiding the performance. The "Thanks but I can't eat that." The "I'm just not hungry." The awkwardness of watching everyone else enjoy a meal while I scan it for landmines—onions, textures, smells, things I can't stomach and don't want to justify for the hundredth time.

That's the part no one talks about: the social cost of not fitting into the default plate.

When I say no to dinner parties, potlucks, or family gatherings, I'm not rejecting the people—I'm sidestepping the discomfort. The looks. The "you're so picky." The comments that sting more than they should because I've heard them all my life.

Over time, this becomes a pattern. You start to anticipate the moment you'll feel out of place, and instead of fighting to belong, you just… disappear.

This is how exclusion works in real time.

Not by pushing people out, but by designing rituals they can't fully join.

We think community happens around the table. But only if the table has room for everyone's appetite, preference, and need—not just the ones that are easiest to accommodate. And when it doesn't? The person with the "weird diet" fades into the background, becomes the "maybe next time," or simply stops showing up altogether.

This is The Onion Effect at scale.

Because every social opt-out isn't just about avoiding onions. It's about avoiding rejection. Avoiding shame. Avoiding being "too much" or "too sensitive." It's survival masquerading as passivity.

So if I didn't come—it wasn't because I didn't care.

It's because I've learned the script, and I'm tired of reciting lines that make other people comfortable while I sit in quiet, hungry dissonance.
