# The Onion Effect – Episode 3
## The "Just Push Through" Myth: When Masking Becomes Survival Mode

You'd be shocked how much of my life I've spent pretending I was okay.

Smiling when I was overstimulated. Sitting through loud restaurants, fake-laughing at jokes I couldn't process fast enough. Showing up when I had nothing left to give—because canceling would mean explaining, and explaining would mean revealing, and revealing would mean risking the label: dramatic, flaky, sensitive, difficult.

So I learned to "just push through."

But here's the thing nobody tells you: pushing through has a cost.

There's a difference between resilience and suppression. One builds you up. The other erodes you. Every time I push through a situation that overwhelms me—socially, mentally, emotionally—I trade a little bit of myself for the comfort of others.

I'm the one who spirals afterward. Who crashes for days. Who lies in bed, replaying the way I laughed too hard, talked too fast, smiled too long—overcompensating so no one would know I was seconds from shutting down.

And for what?

To prove I can keep up with a world that's never slowed down enough to meet me where I am?

That's The Onion Effect too.

It's not just about food. It's about how often we are praised for our performance, and punished for our honesty.

Because when I don't push through—when I cancel, when I go quiet, when I say "I can't today"—I feel like I have to justify it. Like I need a doctor's note or a crisis to validate needing rest.

But rest isn't weak. Boundaries aren't excuses. And "pushing through" isn't always noble. Sometimes it's self-betrayal in a shiny wrapper.

So I'm done clapping for the mask.

I want a life I don't have to recover from.

And if that means disappointing a few people who only loved the version of me that over-delivered and under-communicated? So be it.
