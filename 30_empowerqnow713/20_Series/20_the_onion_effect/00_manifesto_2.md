# The Onion Effect
## Why the Little Things Aren't So Little

We are taught to overlook the small stuff.

Be grateful. Don't be difficult. Don't make a scene. It's just a preference, just a habit, just a detail.

But when the "small stuff" happens every day, it stops being small.

For me, it's onions. Literal onions. I've spent thirty-seven years dodging them—in meals, at parties, in restaurants, and in the homes of well-meaning friends. "You can't even taste them," they say. But I can. I always can. And more importantly, I always have to—explain, defend, mask, smile, decline, or just eat around it while pretending I'm fine.

This isn't about onions.

This is about what I now call **The Onion Effect**.

It's the invisible tax of being different. The slow, grinding friction of living in a world where the defaults don't fit you—and where your preferences are framed as inconveniences. Whether it's food, culture, race, neurodivergence, queerness, gender expression, or chronic illness, every minor accommodation starts to feel like a negotiation.

This series exists to name those negotiations.

To put words to the micro-frictions we normalize and bury.

We'll talk about skipped dinner parties, modified burger orders, people saying "you're so picky," or worse, "you're too sensitive." We'll peel back the layers of what it means to always be adjusting yourself just to exist without being "too much." We'll explore how these little things stack up, shaping our relationships, our choices, and our sense of belonging.

I don't want to keep pretending these moments don't affect me.

I want to trace the patterns. I want to show the map.

Because once we name them, we can challenge them—and maybe even stop blaming ourselves for how exhausting it all feels.

So, welcome to **The Onion Effect**.

It's layered. It's real. And if you've ever felt quietly left out, this one's for you.
