Interview transcript from The Diary of a CEO podcast

## 1. Introduction: Dr. Martha Beck's Mission and Background

### Opening Demonstration

When we lie, our bodies get very weak. For example, stick your arm out and say "I love fresh air, I love fresh air." Now do that while lying—say "I love to vomit." Notice the difference- It's because of the way the brain is structured.

### About Dr. Martha Beck

Martha Beck, PhD, is a Harvard-trained sociologist and world-renowned life coach whose notable clients include Oprah Winfrey. Her neurologically-based techniques have helped individuals cope and adapt to an anxiety-addicted world.

### Her Mission

Our brains were biologically pre-programmed to be anxious, taught by innocently believing lies through socialization or trauma. Socialization says "you're not good enough, you should try harder, that was a bad choice." Trauma tells you "oh my God, everything's dangerous all the time" and creates horror stories that haven't happened yet to make you safe.

The thing about anxiety is that you get stuck in the anxiety spiral—it just keeps getting worse. For example, I have memories and a lot of physical scarring from sexual abuse which started at 5 years old. By the time I was 30, I had depression and anxiety, been bedridden with autoimmune diseases, thinking I could just kill myself. But I can tell you with 100% certainty it is possible to trick our brains and shut down anxiety.

### The Shift in Human Consciousness

Ever since I was little, my whole intent has been based on this feeling that I was meant to help with a shift that would happen in the world during my lifetime. I think there is going to be a shift in the way in human consciousness, and I think it is going to change the way humans relate to the planet, relate to each other, relate to themselves.

The shift is about **Awakening**—awakening out of the dream of thought. It's a shift where you leave the aspects of your thinking that cause you internal suffering. After you awaken, there's no fear, there's no suffering, there is infinite compassion, there is the desire to serve, there is love for all beings—not just every human but every being. There is a kind of fundamental peace and Bliss—the Bliss of being.

### Qualifications

I am a person who has experienced intense psychological and physical suffering for decades—absolute wreck of a human being physically. By the time I was 30, I had been bedridden for 10 years with autoimmune diseases, had depression and anxiety in massive amounts from the time I was very small.

I actually had an experience during a surgery which was like a near-death experience where I felt like I saw this light and I felt connected to it—more than connected to it, I felt radically shifted. I came out of that surgery and changed. I stopped telling a single lie with any aspect of my speech or behavior.

Through everything I have studied with my mind and through everything I've experienced with my body and my heart, I'm not saying I awakened, but I feel I know what Awakening is. For that reason, I feel very safe in the world and very joyful.

## 2. Understanding Anxiety: The Brain, Anxiety Spiral, and Root Causes

### The Anxiety Spiral

Anxiety is like one of those tire rippers that you drive across and you can't drive back. The way the brain is structured, when you get into anxiety, it just keeps going up and up and getting worse and worse.

There's a jolt of fear, then a story about the fear, then there's a story about how we have to control the world so that we won't be in danger anymore. We have to control our loved ones so they won't be in danger. We just have to control—but we honest to God really can't control very much. So then we get worried, we get even more scared, and that feeds back into these primitive brain structures that say fear, and it creates a bigger story and more control efforts, and it goes up and up and up. It doesn't go down because that part of the brain has a very peculiar tendency to truly believe that nothing but itself exists.

### The Brain Structure

You've got your brain, and it's symmetrical. There's something in the middle called the Corpus Callosum that connects it.

**Left Hemisphere (Anxiety Spiral):**

- There's a little tiny part of your brain called the amygdala—very primitive. Every animal with a spine has one. Its job is to make you safe by being alarmed when you see unfamiliar things.
- It feeds information to layers of the brain that are also ancient but not as old. These on the left hemisphere make you immediately start thinking of ways to control a situation.
- When it gets to the outermost layer of the left hemisphere, which handles things like time and language, it starts to tell a story defending the feelings it's having.

**Right Hemisphere (Creativity):**

- On the right side, you also have an amygdala. The right side amygdala also goes "ah, something unfamiliar"—little burst of fear.
- But on the right side, it creates **curiosity** instead of aversion. Have you ever rubber-necked at an accident- Everybody slows down and you're like "what happened, what happened-" The reason is that we evolved a tendency to move away from frightening things to be safe, but toward them insofar as we can figure out what happened and avoid that happening to us.
- Curiosity is intense around things we fear. The right side amygdala goes curious, then it starts to connect things: "how can I figure this out- That's like that other thing, so this is what must have happened." It's a detective.
- It starts to put together its own version of what happened. It doesn't use language but uses very vivid images and sensory details, and it can connect things in ways that are highly original and inventive. You immediately start to get creative.

### The Toggle Effect

Just as anxiety shuts off creativity, **creativity can shut down anxiety**. It's like these two parts of the brain toggle. If you deliberately choose to push your brain toward creativity and get the right side moving, your anxiety shuts down.

### Why Anxiety Exists

Anxiety skyrocketing all over the world—it is by far the most common mental health challenge that people face. Something like 284 million people last I checked were clinically diagnosable with anxiety disorder. During the pandemic year 2020, anxiety went up all over the world by a full 25%.

Jeff Bezos, one of the richest men in the world, says in his quarterly report that he tells all of the thousands of Amazon employees who work under him he wants them all to wake up terrified every morning and to stay terrified all day because that makes them productive. But most of these people are just getting by financially. He wants them to be afraid all the time so that he and the stockholders can get more stuff.

### Fear vs. Anxiety

**Fear** is like being shot from a cannon. If a bear came in here, we would both go "whoa" and we'd get very clear instructions from our biology to either fight, flee, freeze, or hide. And then our fear, if we were like other animals, would subside. That's normal fear.

**Anxiety** instead of being like shot from a cannon, it's like being haunted. Something bad happens or we hear about something bad happening, and we get that jolt of fear, but instead of acting and then relaxing, we turn it into a verbal story.

A group of psychologists in the 90s decided to try to figure out why humans of all animals are the only ones who commit suicide on a regular basis. The answer is **language**. We humans have the capacity to use language to create an abstract vision of the future that is more horrifying than the prospect of our own death. We choose death over the story of fear that we carry in our minds.

## 3. Practical Techniques: Exercises to Reduce Anxiety

### Technique 1: Sensory Imagination (The Orange Exercise)

If you're feeling anxious, here's what to do:

1. Think of something that makes you feel a bit anxious (maybe not panicky, but anxious).
2. Notice what it does to your body and to your emotions. What's happening in your body- Your breath is short, you feel tense, you become quite impatient.
3. Now imagine something else very vividly. Close your eyes. Have you ever eaten an orange-
4. Imagine that you are holding an orange. It's a nice ripe, heavy, delicious orange at the peak of its ripeness. You can smell the citrus. Just take a bite of it to break the seal of the peeling and feel that little spray of citric acid that pops up when you bite the peel, then the bitterness of the rind. As you bite in, the juice gets in your mouth—it's sweet, it's a little bit tangy. You can feel the filaments of the skin and the stringiness of the insides. You pull back the peel, you can feel it on your fingernails, you can smell it. Put the broken part to your mouth and squeeze the orange and let some juice get into your mouth and taste it completely, then swallow it. Enjoy the sensation of tasting, feeling, hearing this experience.

**Result:** Your anxiety went away. Why- Because I asked you to use **sensory imagination**, and that's handled by the right hemisphere. It's not in the left. Instead of verbal imagination which can create horror stories, you were in a sensory experience.

When you imagine forward with your senses in a way that brings relaxation, your body relaxes. You start breathing more deeply. You stop producing all the cortisol, the glucocorticoids, the adrenaline that you had in the fight-flight state. Now you're starting to produce serotonin and dopamine and the "tend and befriend" hormones.

### Technique 2: Mirror Writing (Signature Exercise)

This exercise comes from a brilliant artist and professor at Harvard, William Ryman:

1. Put your stylus over toward the right center of your field of vision and write your first name the way you usually sign it.
2. Now put your stylus just to the left of the signature.
3. Replicate the signature, but this time write it in mirror writing—backwards. Take as much time as you need.

**Why this works:** While you were doing that, you might have felt intense frustration and a sense of "I can't do this." But when you're anxious about it, you actually can't do it. You have to become engrossed with it in order to do it because your brain is creating new neurons, synapses that have never existed before. You've never done this before, so you are fundamentally changing your brain, teaching it a skill it has never had.

This is what children are going through when they learn to write for the first time. What you just did was connect to parts of the brain that are in the right side. It's like powerlifting—you forced your brain to create synapses that were brand new, taking you into a state of deep learning.

**The NASA Study:** There was a study done at NASA in the 60s to identify creative geniuses. They found that 2% of the adults they sought out (like college graduates) were creative geniuses. After a while, a few years, they decided to try giving it to four- and five-year-olds. 98% of them were creative geniuses. What happens between the moment you're four years old (a full-on creative genius learning new things day and day out) and adulthood where your genius has mainly gone dark- It's because you stop trying things that are brand new. You're put in the factory line in school and taught to learn in a completely different way that's based on shame and fear and artificial skills that don't mean much to you.

### Technique 3: The Body Truth Test

Stick your arm out and hold it up. Don't let me push it down. Now do that while lying. The lie I'd like you to say is "I love to vomit." Say it while holding your arm up. Now say "I love fresh air, I love fresh air."

**What happens:** When you said "I love to vomit," I could push your hand down. When you said "I love fresh air," I couldn't push your hand down. Why- Because the body lives in reality. The body is honest. Only the mind, and only the verbal mind, can lie to us and tell us things that we can believe even though they're not true.

This is why polygraph machines work on everybody (except psychopaths). When you lie, your body gets very weak.

### Technique 4: Expressive Writing

There's a psychologist named James Pennebaker who found that if he just had students write for 15 minutes about something that was upsetting to them, many of them came out of the experiment in tears. It really upset them for an hour or two. But in the weeks and even the years subsequent to that experiment, they had fewer doctor's visits, they had less anxiety, they had better relationships, they had better everything.

The parts of us that are frightened need to be heard. Just express yourself—not to show anyone, not even to reread—just to express. The parts of society that are hurting need to be heard.

## 4. The CAT Process: Calm, Art, and Transcendence

### The Three-Step Process

There's a three-step process with the acronym **CAT**: **Calm, Art, and Transcendence**.

### Step 1: Calm

The first third is just how to calm your brain. It's been taught to be anxious. It is biologically pre-programmed to be anxious, so to calm it down.

Most people will say they'll come in and tell me "I want to fight my anxiety, I want to get it, I want to end it, I want to bring it down, I want it gone" because they think it's a broken machine. But it's not a broken machine—**it's a frightened animal**.

If you came in and I said to you "okay, I want to end you, I want to bring you down, I'm going to fight you till you're gone," would you be less afraid or more afraid- So they're attacking the part of themselves that's anxious, and it makes it more anxious.

**We have to be gentle with ourselves.** We are taught to be violent to ourselves—"biohack that, make yourself eat this and do that." Instead, if we could just go to the anxious part and say "I'm going to give you space. I'm here, I'm going to be here with you. I know she's scaring you, but I've got you. It's okay. She's not going to hurt us. I can go in the other room with you if you need." Sit with it and say "let me know what you're feeling. Tell me everything. You get to feel exactly the way you feel, and I'm here to listen to anything you want to tell me. I will not hurt you, and I will not try to stifle you or make you go away."

When you do this, the volume goes down. The anxiety subsides.

### Step 2: Art (Creativity)

Once you get really calm, then very paradoxically, you need **Art**. I don't mean drawing—I mean making things. Making things in three dimensions, making events happen, making a podcast.

**The Magic of Creating:** If people have been through a trauma and they're allowed to draw about it (even if they can't draw professionally), they have an 80% lower chance of developing PTSD. There's something about creating stuff.

It could be a company, or it could be spray paint on a cardboard. My partner started making bead bracelets a while ago. She's very busy, she doesn't have time for this, but it makes her so content.

If you go into a tomb in Egypt from 5,000 years ago, what are you going to find- Among other things, beaded bracelets. If you go to the Amazon rainforest and contact an uncontacted tribe, what might you find- Beaded bracelets. People are making beaded bracelets all the time, and they serve no function. They are precious, pointless things that we make, and all cultures make.

We make music. I think about the cultures in Jamaica—one of the worst slavery colonies in the history of the world. Out of that, you get these incredible art forms: reggae, dance. In the middle of being crushed, having literally everything taken from them, people were still making art. This is a part of the human spirit that is just indomitable.

**How it works:** Because of the way the structures on the left side are obsessed with grasping material objects, acquiring, controlling other people, always thinking about fear—there does seem to be this toggle effect that anxiety and creativity just can't work at the same time. So the moment you begin to create, the toggle switches off in anxiety and on in creativity.

I believe that there's another spiral on the right side of the brain, but instead of spiraling tightly into fear, it spirals outward. And ultimately, you get to the final thing.

### Step 3: Transcendence (Awakening)

There's calming, there's Artistry, and then there's **Transcendence** or **Awakening**.

Sometimes we call it **Flow**. Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, the psychologist who named it Flow, really looked into this. It's a state of creating and performing at a level so difficult we almost can't do it (exactly the way you were writing your name). It's like "I can't, but when you get it..."

When you get it, it's like flying. It's heaven. There's a time in the process of creation where the sense of self falls away and the sense of control isn't necessary. What you feel is creation itself sort of moving with you and through you, and it's blissful.

I believe that is the state in which we are meant to spend almost all our time. I think that would transform our consciousness.

## 5. Personal Journey: Trauma, Recovery, and Transformation

### Early Life and Mormon Upbringing

I was born not just into a Mormon family but a Mormon community where everyone shared the same beliefs. You didn't call people "Mr." and "Mrs."—it was "brother" and "sister." Brother Smith, Sister Smith.

I was told from very young—you're indoctrinated at 18 months. You start religious training, and they tell you things like: "If men live well and they're part of the Mormon church, then when they die they get their own planet and all the women they want." And "Jesus is going to come over the mountains and all the graves are going to fly open and all the bodies—the literal bodies of all the dead people—are going to rise up out and go join Jesus."

I would have nightmares of Jesus coming over the mountains, the graves flying open, all the people around me rising up. I would run as a little kid. This happened over and over again—this dream where I was trying to jump high enough to go with the people who were being saved, and I couldn't do it. I just kept coming back down.

I lived in absolute terror all the time. I also didn't know what was real because none of it felt real. It's very disconcerting, but because I'd never had any other experience, I just thought "well, this is life."

### The Abuse

At 28 years old, I realized that I'd been sexually assaulted as a child. I think I had hints of it. Friends told me that I had told them about it in high school, and I don't remember telling them, so I had pretty much repressed it.

My father was a very, very renowned scholarly defender of Mormonism. His job was to take the claims of the doctrine and validate them academically. But in order to do that, I talked to many people who worked with him, and they said he would just make things up and put them as footnotes in different languages so no one was likely to check them. It was called "lying for the Lord."

I do have memories and a lot of physical scarring from sexual abuse. The surgery I was in when I had the light experience was surgery to correct some of the scar tissue left by the abuse. I was bleeding internally, and they just found all this scar tissue where it probably shouldn't have been.

### The Light Experience

During a surgery, I had an experience which was like a near-death experience. I'm in surgery, they're operating on me. I look around, I sit up, and then I think "why am I sitting up- I'm having surgery." I look down—there's my body, they're operating on it. I was like "this is weird." So I lay back down.

There were bright surgical lights, and the light that appeared between them was just small at first, like a golf ball. They tell us we only see a trillionth of the available light spectrum. We only see a trillionth of the colors that exist. I think I could see trillions more colors than I'd ever seen before, and it was absolutely mesmerizing. You could not—you would never want to look away from it.

Then it got bigger, and in my case, it touched my body, and this feeling of absolute exquisite joy just coursed through me. It was the realest thing I'd ever seen—so much realer than the body that was being operated on. It was laughing with joy, and I was laughing with joy. I started to cry because I was pure relief, pure happiness.

The moment I woke up, I was like "bring me the anesthesiologist, please." Actually, I couldn't stop crying for hours because I loved everyone so much. Everybody that was there—there was a janitor—I was like "I love you so much."

They brought me the anesthesiologist, and he seemed terrified. He was afraid that he'd done something wrong. I said "what did you give me- What are the side effects- What happens to people under this surgery- What goes on-" He said "just tell me what happened."

I said "what do you mean-" He said "well, I was going to give you more medication, and then a voice said 'don't do that, she's crying because she's happy.'" He said "I just listened to it, and I don't know why." He was like "did I do the right thing-"

I told him a little bit, and he said "do you know how many times this has happened to me in 33 years of giving people anesthesia-" I said "how many-" He said "once." Then he gave me a kiss on the forehead and went away.

I don't think it was a drug effect.

### The Vow of Truth

From that experience, I made a vow not to lie anymore—not with any aspect of my speech or behavior. I would not lie after that.

The reason was I had heard "the truth will set you free." I had studied so many wisdom traditions, looking everywhere for a reason not to commit suicide. I knew a lot of religious texts, philosophical texts. I had done my homework, and over and over and over and over, it said "the truth will set you free."

In Mormonism, they said "the truth is what we've written down here," and it was bogus and phony. But the light was far more true than anything else I'd ever experienced. It was far more real. So I was like "okay, if truth takes me there..."

It told me (not verbally, but it said): "Look, you've been thinking that you could kill yourself and feel better. I am telling you that you are meant to learn to feel this way—the way you feel with me now—when you're alive, always. So go and do that."

What I really did was I made it—it wasn't even a choice, it was an absolute obsession. I would not live in such a way that I was not conscious of the presence of that light. And that meant every time I lied, I felt it withdraw. You can't withdraw from it—it's everywhere, I believe—but I felt myself less conscious of it. I was like "okay, that's not going to work."

So I decided: "What I'm going to do is I'm just going to say what's real, do what's real. If a thought comes in that feels like it's pulling me away from that light, I will question that thought. It can't be real. It doesn't set me free. It doesn't bring me into that. I'm going to investigate everything until I find what feels truest to me."

### The Consequences of Truth

In the next year, that was a very exciting year:

- I walked away from my family religion, which was very, very important in my home community. That meant I lost my family of origin, my community of origin, every friend I'd had before the age of 17 when I left for college.
- I realized I was gay, so I left—that was the end of my marriage.
- I had to leave my home.
- I left Academia. Basically threw everything into the bonfire.

I would not recommend this to anyone listening out there. Don't do this. I did this so you would not have to. I can tell you there are easier ways.

### Confronting the Abuse

I called my mother and told her about the abuse. She said "well yes, that's what happened." I was like "what, you agree with me-" She said "why shouldn't I- I know him better than you."

I said "okay, so like what do I do-" She said "well, obviously you have to protect the church." She said "you better come and make him a cake."

For a mother to say "oh yeah, I completely believe that's true, and what I think you should do about it is to make your perpetrator a cake" kind of sums up the way I was raised.

I tried. I made the cake. I went down. I served the cake. Then I just couldn't go back. I just couldn't.

I confronted him. Years later, 10 years later or so, when he was 90-91 (I was born when he was 52), I wanted to meet with him after I'd forgiven him to tell him that I'd forgiven him so that he would not have to carry that. He was a very, very miserable, strange, dissociated human being—really, really weird. People—he was brilliant, but very, very, very broken.

I think he had to choose between his entire sense of reality and his religion, and he chose the religion. He chose the job of talking other people into believing the religion, and I think it just completely broke him.

Did he acknowledge that he had done it- No. It's very strange about it, though. He didn't say "I never did that." He said "oh, but that was the evil one," meaning the devil. That was my family—that I'd been sexually assaulted by the devil as a child, and that's why I had scars.

### Transformation

From the person I was at 19 years old (constantly thinking about suicide) to the person I am now—if I met that teenager and she sat down here, I would just go back for the first time in years. I had a gig in Boston, so I went back to Cambridge. I went with my wife (something that couldn't have happened when I was 17). I had the sense of tapping my younger self on the shoulder and saying "I am from your future, and I can tell you with 100% certainty that it is possible for you to live in a state of almost continuous joy, and that you can get there without dying. You can get there. In fact, your job in this world is to find a way to live in a state of continuous joy without dying."

If she turned to me and said "Dr. Martha, what is step one-" I would say: "Sit down with yourself and find a part of you that can say to your suffering (which is huge): 'I love you. It'll be okay. I'm right here.'"

That's something I call internal self-talk. The acronym is KISSED. I didn't tell anyone about it for decades because it's so corny-sounding, but that one thing—in Tibetan Buddhism, they might call it the basis of loving kindness. For years, sometimes the monks who are trained there and the nuns will sit in meditation for days and days and do nothing but offer kindness to themselves.

It has to start that way. You sit with your miserable self, and you say: "May you be happy. May you be free from suffering. May you feel safe and protected. May you live with ease." As I offer her those wishes, I become the part of myself that is real because the suffering is the part of the dream world, and the reality is infinitely loving and intelligent beyond—so far beyond—our silly monkey minds.

## 6. Finding Purpose: Meaning, Ubuntu, and Deep Gladness

### The Universal Question

Almost everyone has the same major problem, and it's not what you would think. They want to know their purpose. They want to know why the hell they're even here.

Humans are the only animals, so far as we know, that live on a day-to-day basis with the consciousness of our mortality. We are going to die, so why are we even here- What am I doing here-

It's the same whether you're talking to someone on the street or someone with a billion dollars. That desperation to know why we're here comes out of a culture that has fundamentally pulled us away from our inherent knowledge of what we're meant to be and put us in a place where we are obsessed with productivity and consumption and production of material wealth. It has actually cut us off from our own sense of meaning.

### The Left Hemisphere Trap

You get stuck in a part of the left hemisphere that is obsessed with grabbing things and owning things and controlling things. It's always afraid. It's always grasping, and it refuses to believe that anything but itself exists.

But on the other side of the brain, there is the self that connects with meaning, purpose, relationship, connection, and living in a state of nature as everyone did until a few hundred years ago.

### Our Natural State

Almost everyone would wake up hearing wind and bird song and other people's voices. They would rise and go to bed according to the sunlight and the temperature. They had intimate relationships with animals and with plants and with the Earth itself. All of our biology evolved to be in that situation.

We in one anthropologist called it the WEIRD societies—Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic. We have a fundamentally different way of living. We get up surrounded by artificial light. We push ourselves all day to do things that we would never have done 300 years ago—spreadsheets, sitting next to people we barely know who are assigned to be there because we have similar tasks (which is a system based on factory labor, which is horrible for people).

Not to solve real problems that matter to you, but to catch on to something that an adult already knows. Who's going to punish you or shame you depending on whether you get the right answer or the wrong- It's a bizarre, very left-hemisphere-dominated society.

Ian McGilchrist, my favorite philosopher (a neurologist/psychiatrist), says the whole culture functions like someone with a severe right-hemisphere stroke. We live in a bizarre, crazy culture, and we do not know why we're here because we don't have access to our sense of meaning.

### Ubuntu: I Am Because We Are

The meaning of life is not what happens to people. The meaning of life, your purpose in life, is **what happens between people**.

There's a concept in South Africa called **Ubuntu**. There's no English translation, and it is completely the opposite of our cultural individualism. The meaning of Ubuntu is basically: **"I am me because we are us."** I am fundamentally different because I know you, and you matter to me.

I used to be confused in South Africa because I knew there were a lot of AIDS orphans, and I never saw them on the streets or anything. Then I realized that Ubuntu is a real practical thing there. The children who are left are absorbed into community by people who may have nothing except Ubuntu.

There's a Chinese proverb that says: "If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together." We've been going really fast in this culture—fast toward our own destruction.

### The Space Between

You can do an exercise to get into your right hemisphere. We're looking at each other, but if you look—without moving your eyes—look at the distance between us. Look at the openness between us. You feel how it changes your gaze, how it changes your heartbeat.

Carl Jung, the psychologist, had a dear friend who was a Pueblo Indian. He said "what do you really think of us Anglos-" He said "we think you're insane." He said "why-" This guy's name was Chief Mountain Lake. He said: "You're always staring at things, and yet you never see each other. You never see what's between you. Our eyes are soft, and yours are hard."

When you do that exercise, my whole body went into a state. It's like the light—I'm more conscious of it when I'm looking at the space between us, and I feel you. I don't just see you. I felt like my heart rate dropped. I just felt really calm.

### Deep Gladness Meets Deep Hunger

Your purpose in life is where your **deep gladness** and the **world's deep hunger** meet.
