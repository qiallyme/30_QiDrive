03-01 – Building Systems for Neurodivergent Minds

This was the beginning of my ascension—not the clean, Instagrammable kind. Not the “new chapter” people post about after a breakup or a bad haircut. This was the kind that happens when you’re buried so deep in survival mode that you have no choice but to evolve or disappear. My body was breaking, my spirit was fraying, and my mind… my mind was screaming for order in a world that felt like chaos wearing a business suit.

So I built a system.

Not because I wanted to be productive. Because I had to. Because my ADHD was turning every hour into a fog of half-finished thoughts and forgotten promises. Because PTSD made even the smallest decisions feel like landmines. Because no one around me had the capacity or the patience to understand what it meant to be brilliant and broken at the same time.

I used every tool I could get my hands on.

Notion became my second brain. Scripts and automations became my co-workers. I turned task management into a kind of sacred ritual—breaking down chaos into color-coded peace. I wasn’t just organizing files. I was organizing my mind. I built flows that caught me when I was spiraling. Templates that reminded me who I was when depression tried to rewrite the script. Workspaces that made sense when nothing else did.

People thought I was a machine.

A genius. A superhero. The one who could handle everything, fix everything, carry everything. They didn’t see the panic attacks between calls, the way I’d cry over a single misplaced email, or how hard I clung to these systems because they were the only thing keeping me from drowning. I wasn’t trying to impress anyone—I was trying to survive my own wiring in a world not built for it.

This was never about hustle. It was about translation.

Taking a mind that sees everything all at once and building filters that let me move through the day without collapsing. I created frameworks that turned overwhelm into action. I wasn’t Superman—I was just a man refusing to let his pain be the end of the story. My systems weren’t pretty. But they worked. And for the first time in a long time, so did I.
