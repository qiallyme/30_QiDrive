> So she just had an emergency was very close to emergency need maybe fatal nobody was here chair died and no oxygen within reach but she made it to her home unit now she is talking so fast and is all over the place like she is high but she’s not

### Assistant
> Stop. Breathe. We triage this logically.
> 
> What you just described is a **near hypoxic episode**.
> 
> If:
> - Her chair died  
> - Oxygen was not within reach  
> - She was close to respiratory distress  
> 
> And now she is:
> - Talking very fast  
> - All over the place  
> - Acting “high”  
> 
> That is **not normal post-event behavior**.
> 
> This can be:
> - Residual hypoxia (low oxygen to brain)
> - CO₂ retention (especially if she has COPD)
> - Adrenaline dump after near-panic
> - Early respiratory failure rebound
> - Cardiac stress
> - Electrolyte shift
> 
> You do NOT assume she’s fine because she’s talking.
> 
> Fast, pressured, erratic speech after oxygen deprivation is a red flag.
> 
> I need you to check right now:
> 
> 1. Is she on oxygen currently?
> 2. What is her pulse ox reading?
> 3. Is she confused about where she is or what happened?
> 4. Is her breathing labored?
> 5. Is her heart racing?
> 6. Is her skin pale, gray, or bluish?
> 
> If her oxygen saturation is under 90% → call 911.
> If she is mentally altered → call 911.
> If she looks “not baseline” → call 911.
> 
> You do not get judged for calling.
> You get judged for not calling.
> 
> You already had a near fatal scenario.
> 
> This is not the moment to “wait and see.”
> 
> Answer me:
> What is her oxygen saturation right now?

### User

### Assistant
> Maybe.  
> 
> But you do **not** get to diagnose “adrenaline dump” right after a near hypoxic event without data.
> 
> Adrenaline dump:
> - Fast talking  
> - Shaky  
> - Emotional  
> - Wide eyes  
> - Heart racing  
> - But oriented and making sense  
> 
> Hypoxia / CO₂ retention:
> - Fast or weird speech  
> - Confusion  
> - Rambling  
> - Personality shift  
> - Headache  
> - Drowsy after the surge  
> - Not fully tracking  
> 
> These can look similar in the first 10–20 minutes.
> 
> We don’t guess. We verify.
> 
> Right now:
> 
> 1. What is her pulse ox number?
> 2. Is she answering questions clearly and correctly?
>    - What day is it?
>    - Where are we?
>    - What just happened?
> 3. Is she more energized or drifting?
> 
> If her O2 is solid (≥92% for her baseline) and she’s oriented, this may very well be adrenaline.
> 
> If her O2 is borderline or she’s cognitively off → this is medical.
> 
> Near-fatal + oxygen interruption = you err on the side of emergency.
> 
> You need objective numbers, not vibes.
> 
> Tell me her oxygen reading.
