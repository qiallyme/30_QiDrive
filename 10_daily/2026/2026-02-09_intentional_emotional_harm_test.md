### Assistant
> Short answer: **maybe, but not the way you’re framing it right now.**  
> This is where precision matters.
> 
> You’re trying to jump straight to **“intentional emotional harm.”** Courts almost never accept that leap just from rude or dismissive behavior. You need to ground it in the legal lane it actually fits.
> 
> Let’s slow this down and put this in the right bucket.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## First — what NotebookLM meant by “intentional infliction of emotional harm”
> 
> That phrase maps to the tort **Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED).**
> 
> The legal bar for IIED is *extremely high*.  
> You must show conduct that is:
> 
> 1. **Extreme and outrageous**  
> 2. **Intentional or reckless**  
> 3. **Caused severe emotional distress**
> 
> Key reality:  
> Courts do **not** consider someone being rude, unsympathetic, or cold as “outrageous.”  
> Even very mean behavior often fails this test.
> 
> So if your argument is:
> 
> > She didn’t care and had an attitude.
> 
> That alone → **not enough.**
> 
> But don’t panic — because the video *can still be valuable*, just not as a standalone IIED claim.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## The correct way to use the video
> 
> The recording is powerful **context evidence**, not a magic standalone claim.
> 
> What the video *can* help prove:
> 
> ### 1) Knowledge of your situation
> If she knew:
> - you had no notice
> - you were being displaced suddenly
> - you were asking for time or communication
> - you were trying to resolve things
> 
> Then her comments help show **they knew the consequences of their actions.**
> 
> This matters for:
> - bad faith
> - recklessness
> - punitive damages
> - credibility
> - pattern of conduct
> 
> This is MUCH stronger legally.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 2) Reckless disregard (this is the real gold)
> 
> Courts care deeply about **reckless disregard**.
> 
> That means:
> > They knew harm would happen and did it anyway.
> 
> Examples that would help:
> - “You had your chance, not my problem”
> - “There’s nothing we can do”
> - “We don’t care”
> - “You need to leave”
> - dismissing requests for time, notice, or communication
> 
> Why this matters:
> 
> Because your case already includes:
> - disputed service
> - sudden eviction/writ
> - lack of notice
> - continued credit reporting harm
> 
> So the video becomes proof of the **human side of the institutional conduct.**
> 
> It supports a narrative of:
> > Not a mistake. A pattern of disregard.
> 
> Huge difference.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 3) It shows state of mind (very important)
> 
> Courts and juries care about **state of mind.**
> 
> The video can demonstrate:
> - dismissiveness
> - indifference
> - lack of investigation
> - lack of willingness to correct
> 
> This helps with:
> - punitive damages
> - credibility attacks
> - bad faith arguments
> - pattern of conduct
> 
> It strengthens the *story of the case.*
> 
> And cases are won on **stories supported by evidence.**
> 
> ---
> 
> ## Where this actually fits in your claims
> 
> This video supports:
> 
> - Bad faith conduct
> - Reckless disregard for consequences
> - Negligent / reckless servicing
> - Pattern of harm
> - Punitive damages arguments
> 
> It **supports emotional distress damages**, but does not prove IIED by itself.
> 
> Think:
> > Supporting pillar, not the entire building.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## The strategic framing you want
> 
> Not:
> > She intended to emotionally harm me.
> 
> Instead:
> > The recording demonstrates the institution’s awareness of the harm their actions were causing and their reckless disregard for the consequences.
> 
> That wording is powerful and believable.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## Reality check
> 
> You don’t need to prove she wanted to hurt you emotionally.
> 
> You need to show:
> - they knew harm would happen
> - they didn’t care
> - they kept going anyway
> 
> That’s a much stronger and more winnable lane.
>
