> ## The strongest filing sequence
> 
> ### 1) Main motion first
> **Verified Motion for Relief from Void Judgment, to Vacate Derivative Orders, and for Stay Pending Disposition**
> 
> Use **Trial Rule 60(B)(6)** as the lead lane for a **void judgment**, with **60(B)(3)** and **60(B)(8)** pleaded in the alternative for misrepresentation / misconduct and other extraordinary reasons. > 
> **Ask to vacate:**
> - default judgment
> - decree of foreclosure
> - order confirming sale, if applicable
> - sheriff’s deed consequences / derivative title effects to extent within trial court power
> - writ / possession-related derivative orders
> - any later orders built on the defective foundation
> 
> **Core theory:**
> This is not “I disagree with the result.”  
> This is: **the record does not contain the service proof the rules contemplate, the court acted without a constitutionally reliable notice foundation, and every downstream order is infected.**
> 
> That is the lane.
> 
> ## 2) Separate enforcement motion
> **Verified Motion to Enforce Prior Order Regarding Surplus, for Rule to Show Cause, and for Sanctions**
> 
> Do **not** bury the surplus issue inside the vacatur motion.
> 
> Make it its own problem:
> - there was an order
> - they had to comply
> - they did not
> - they kept the court in the dark or failed to act
> - sanctions / show cause / deadline / accounting are now warranted
> 
> This one is simpler and cleaner. Judges understand **“I gave an order; why was it not followed?”**
> 
> ## 3) Separate preservation / spoliation motion or supplement
> **Verified Motion for Preservation of Evidence and Sanctions for Spoliation / Failure to Preserve**
> 
> Only file this **if you can identify the evidence with precision**:
> - what evidence
> - who had control
> - when the duty to preserve attached
> - what was lost / altered / withheld
> - how that prejudices you
> 
> If you cannot name the evidence specifically, do **not** lead with “spoliation.”  
> Then it becomes a preservation motion first, sanctions second.
> 
> ## 4) Keep damages theories in reserve
> These are **real leverage**, but they are **not the first hammer** in trial court:
> 
> - conversion
> - illegal lockout / wrongful dispossession
> - damage to property
> - FCRA / credit reporting
> - ADA / civil-rights claims
> - constitutional due process damages
> 
> Use them in the motion package as:
> - background prejudice
> - evidence of ongoing harm
> - reason for expedited hearing
> - reason settlement exposure is increasing
> 
> But do **not** let them swallow the jurisdictional core.
> 
> ## Why the service issue matters
> 
> Indiana’s rules are not casual about mail service. Rule 4.11 says that when service is by certified or registered mail, the return must show the mailing details and include a copy of the return receipt, and that return with receipt becomes part of the record. Rule 4.1(C) also requires notice to the court when service is predicated on mail and the mailed copy is returned before judgment or within thirty days after. citeturn751964view1turn751964view2
> 
> That gives you a clean line:
> 
> > If they are asking the court to rely on mail-based service, the record should contain the mail-based artifacts the rules require.
> 
> That is much better than “trust me, they’re shady.”
> 
> ## Another point you should use
> 
> Under Indiana Trial Rule 5(G)(2), **orders in Trial Rule 69 foreclosure sales are distributed for service by the party who submitted the proposed order**. That matters because it narrows responsibility for service of foreclosure-sale orders and gives you a more specific record-based criticism when order-service was sloppy, missing, or unverifiable. citeturn751964view3
> 
> ## The tone you want
> 
> Not this:
> 
> > Judge, you are obligated as a matter of constitutional law to not allow this to stand. Period.
> 
> Use this instead:
> 
> > Once the corrected record is considered, the issue is not whether Plaintiff can continue to assert that notice occurred. > 
> That is colder, smarter, and harder to swat away.
>
