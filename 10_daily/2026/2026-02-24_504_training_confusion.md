> Cody… “504 training.” I swear property managers just throw numbers around like they’re seasoning. 🍿
> 
> Let’s untangle this calmly and surgically.
> 
> First: what she’s probably confusing.
> 
> There are three different legal buckets people mix up:
> 
> 1) Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act  
> 2) The Fair Housing Act (FHA)  
> 3) The ADA  
> 
> Section 504 applies when the housing provider receives federal financial assistance. Not just “they take Section 8 tenants.” Not just “they accept vouchers.” It specifically applies if the property itself receives federal funding. If it does, they have additional nondiscrimination and accommodation obligations. But there is no such thing as a “504 training you have to take” as a tenant or caregiver. That’s absurd. Section 504 governs the housing provider’s conduct, not yours.
> 
> So if she said you have to “do a 504 training,” that is likely someone misunderstanding compliance requirements that apply to staff, not residents.
> 
> Now let’s separate the two issues she bundled together:
> 
> 1. You as a live-in caregiver  
> 2. Your service animal  
> 
> Different legal analyses.
> 
> Live-in caregiver  
> Under the Fair Housing Act, a disabled tenant can request a reasonable accommodation to allow a live-in aide or caregiver. That is absolutely standard. The landlord can request reliable disability-related documentation if the disability or need is not obvious. They cannot demand invasive medical details. They cannot require the caregiver to “qualify” through some training. They can require basic screening consistent with other occupants, like background check if uniformly applied. They can document that the caregiver has no independent tenancy rights. That’s it.
> 
> Service animal  
> Under the FHA, service animals and emotional support animals are both “assistance animals.” There is no certification requirement. No training certificate requirement. No vest requirement. No registration. None of that is legally required.
> 
> If the disability and need are not obvious, they can request a letter from a healthcare provider stating:
> - The person has a disability (without diagnosis details)
> - The animal provides support or performs tasks related to that disability
> 
> That’s it.
> 
> They cannot require:
> - 504 training
> - ADA certification
> - Proof of “hardening training”
> - Registration
> - Payment of pet fees
> 
> If this is HUD-subsidized housing, they are under FHA and likely Section 504 obligations. Which actually increases their compliance burden, not yours.
> 
> Now here’s the strategic part.
> 
> You do not argue “she qualifies technically.”  
> You do not argue training sufficiency.  
> You do not argue vest status.
>

>

>

>
