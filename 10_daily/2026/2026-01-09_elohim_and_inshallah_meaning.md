> What does Elohim and Inshallah mean in possibly Arabic or Pashto or another dialect from the region?

### Assistant
> Great question! Both **Elohim** and **Inshallah** are deeply spiritual words but come from different linguistic and cultural roots.
> 
> **Elohim** is a Hebrew word. It’s one of the names used for God in the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament). While not Arabic or Pashto, it’s found across Abrahamic traditions. Interestingly, it's a *plural* form (grammatically), but it's often used in a *singular* sense when referring to the one God of Israel. It conveys majesty and reverence.
> 
> **Inshallah** (إن شاء الله) is Arabic and translates to **"If God wills"** or **"God willing."** It’s super common in Muslim communities and is used in both **Arabic** and **Pashto**-speaking regions. In **Pashto**, the word is often said just as it is in Arabic—**Inshallah**—since religious vocabulary is often shared across Islamic cultures.
> 
> So in short:
> - **Elohim** = Hebrew, means “God”
> - **Inshallah** = Arabic (also used in Pashto), means “If God wills”
