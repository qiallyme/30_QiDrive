> Yes, banks and mortgage servicers face significant financial and structural pressures that strongly incentivize rushing or cutting corners to get defaulted loans off their books.
> 
> **The High Cost of Defaulted Loans**
> Servicing a non-performing loan is substantially more expensive than servicing a performing one. When a borrower defaults, the servicer stops receiving their servicing fee income, but they are often still required to advance principal and interest payments to secondary market investors out of their own pockets. Because the servicer also has to absorb additional administrative and property repair costs, there is a massive financial incentive to liquidate the property (via foreclosure or short sale) as quickly as possible to stop the bleeding. 
> 
> **Resource Strain**
> The mortgage industry is highly cyclical. When defaults rise, servicers are routinely caught without enough trained staff or resources to handle the workload. This severe resource constraint can handicap their ability to follow procedures properly, leading to systemic corner-cutting and poor performance—much like the widespread servicing failures seen during the 2008 financial crisis.
> 
> **End-of-Period Target Pressures**
> Banking personnel frequently alter their behavior to meet volume-based targets before a reporting period closes. For example, studies on loan officers show that they become significantly more aggressive and lower their standards toward the end of the month specifically to hit volume quotas and secure compensation. Similar pressures apply to clearing bad debt off the balance sheet before quarterly or annual financial reporting periods.
> 
> **Relevance to Your Case**
