That supports your “lack of meaningful notice / prejudice” argument. citeturn212850search1

> ## Bucket B — Delay damages
> 
> These are the losses caused because you did not have the surplus when you should have had it.

> | Late fees | rent, utilities, loans, phone, car insurance | bills showing due date + late fee | Strong |

> ## Bucket C — Caregiving impact
> 
> This matters because your mother’s care gives the delay a human consequence, but we must document it carefully.
> 
> | Damage Type | Example | Proof Needed | Strength |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Medication / supply delay | unable to buy OTC meds, pill organizer items, supplies | receipts, pharmacy records, care notes | Strong |
> | Missed caregiving stabilization | inability to buy groceries, hygiene supplies, transport | daily caregiver notes | Medium to strong |
> | Increased caregiver burden | extra time managing crisis due to lack of funds | care logs, calendar entries | Medium |
> | Missed paid caregiving income | could not stabilize household/work because funds withheld | schedule + expected pay | Medium |
> | Health/safety risk | food, utilities, mobility limitations | documentation, photos, care notes | Medium |

> 
> > “The delay impaired my ability to provide stable caregiving support and household necessities.”

> ## Bucket D — Lost income / lost opportunity
> 
> This can work, but it has to be tight. Courts hate speculative numbers.
> 
> | Type | Example | Proof Needed | Strength |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Missed work days | could not drive/work due to crisis, lack of gas, lack of stability | calendar, app history, messages | Medium |
> | Lost rideshare income | could not rent car / buy gas / operate | prior earning history, rental quote, app screenshots | Medium |
> | Lost consulting/admin income | time spent filing, correcting, chasing funds | invoices, client messages, time log | Medium |
> | Missed setup opportunity | unable to fund tools/transport needed to earn | quote + proof of expected income | Weak to medium |
> 
> Use conservative math.
> 
> Bad:
> 
> > “They cost me my whole business.”
> 
> Good:
> 
> > “Between May __ and May __, I lost approximately __ workdays. Based on prior average earnings of $__ per day, estimated lost income is $__.”
>

> ## Bucket F — Emotional distress / personal harm
> 
> This is real, but legally harder. Use it as supporting context unless you later have a solid claim path.
> 
> | Harm | Proof | Strength |
> |---|---|---|
> | anxiety, sleep disruption, panic, overwhelm | journal notes, messages, medical notes if any | Medium |
> | humiliation / displacement stress | timeline, witness statements | Medium |
> | caregiver burnout | care logs, messages, witness notes | Medium |
> | physical deterioration from stress | medical records needed | Harder |
> 
> Don’t lead with this. Use it after financial harm.
> 
> Best wording:
> 
> > “The delay caused severe emotional distress and destabilized Defendant’s ability to maintain food, caregiving, utilities, and basic household operations.”
> 
> Do **not** make it the center unless you have medical documentation.

> ## Bucket G — Procedural prejudice
> 
> This is not always a separate dollar amount, but it supports the demand.
> 
> | Event | Harm |
> |---|---|
> | short-notice hearing | deprived meaningful opportunity to appear |
> | repeated hearing despite accepted amount | extended delay |
> | no prompt order | funds remain inaccessible |
> | delay after prior hearing | increased cascading harm |
> 
> This becomes your “why they had notice / why the delay was unreasonable” section.
> 
> Indiana Trial Rule 7 gives courts discretion to set motions for hearing, but your complaint is not “a hearing can never happen.” Your complaint is: **once the matter was narrow and accepted, the short-notice / repeated-hearing process created avoidable prejudice.** Trial Rule 6 gives you the cleaner notice angle. citeturn212850search1

> # 4. Strength ranking
> 
> ## Strong damages
> 
> These are your “lead with these” items:
> 
> 1. surplus amount withheld  
> 2. late fees  
> 3. interest charges  
> 4. overdraft fees  
> 5. borrowing costs  
> 6. actual receipts for food / meds / caregiving supplies  
> 7. filing costs / postage / printing  
> 8. documented missed hearing due to short notice  
> 
> ## Medium damages
> 
> Use, but document tightly:
> 
> 1. missed work  
> 2. lost rideshare / gig income  
> 3. caregiving disruption  
> 4. household instability  
> 5. admin time  
> 
> ## Weak unless documented
> 
> Do not lead with these:
> 
> 1. emotional distress alone  
> 2. generalized suffering  
> 3. “they ruined my life” style claims  
> 4. speculative business loss  
> 5. punitive damages talk before you have a clear claim path  
> 
> Punitive damages are not impossible in some civil contexts, but they are harder and usually require more than “they delayed and caused harm.” Keep your future claim language broad: **damages, costs, interest, statutory remedies, equitable relief, sanctions if available, and any other relief.**

> # 7. The uncomfortable truth
> 
> The bank may not be liable for **every** delay once the money is sitting with the clerk and court direction is required. That matters. Don’t ignore it.
> 
> Your future damages theory needs to separate:
> 
> ## Bank-caused harm
> 
> - inflated figures  
> - inconsistent accounting  
> - improper filings  
> - failure to promptly resolve surplus  
> - bad payoff numbers  
> - notice/service defects if tied to Plaintiff’s actions  
> - misleading court filings  
> - unnecessary resistance or silence  
> 
> ## Court/clerk process harm
> 
> - delayed hearing  
> - delayed signed order  
> - check issuance lag  
> - short-notice setting  
> 
> You usually cannot sue “the court” casually for docket delay. That’s a swamp. So for the bank, the stronger theory is:
> 
> > Their conduct created, extended, or failed to correct the conditions that caused the surplus to remain unpaid.
> 
> That’s the clean lane.
