### Assistant
> You’re absolutely right to be paranoid about this.  
> This is a **VERY real gap**: someone can have authority to *make decisions* but still get blocked from *seeing records* if the wording isn’t explicit enough. Hospitals do this constantly.
> 
> Here’s the clean, Indiana-specific breakdown.
> 
> ---
> 
> # The big picture (Indiana reality)
> In Indiana you need **TWO separate authorities**:
> 
> 1) **Appointment of Health Care Representative / Medical POA**
>    → lets you *make medical decisions*.
> 
> 2) **HIPAA Authorization / Release**
>    → lets you *see and receive medical records*.
> 
> You can combine them in one document, but the HIPAA language must be **explicit and intentional**.
> 
> Why? Because HIPAA is federal privacy law and providers are extremely risk-averse.
> 
> ---
> 
> # The key Indiana statute you care about
> This is the sentence that matters most legally:
> 
> > A health care provider shall give an attorney in fact the same access as the principal to examine and copy medical records. citeturn0search4
> 
> BUT — and this is the trap — providers still often require **explicit HIPAA language** naming the agent as a *personal representative*.
> 
> HIPAA itself says a personal representative has the same access rights as the patient. citeturn0search9
> 
> So your document must clearly create that relationship.
> 
> ---
> 
> # The EXACT wording concept Indiana forms use
> Indiana’s own state sample forms include language like:
> 
> > “I designate my Health Care Representative as my personal representative with respect to all medical information, including protected health information.”
> 
> That phrase is gold. That is the magic phrase. citeturn0search0
> 
> If this language (or very similar) is missing, hospitals get weird.
> 
> ---
> 
> # Required HIPAA authorization elements (federal + Indiana use)
> A valid HIPAA release must contain these **core elements**: citeturn0search14
> 
> - What info can be disclosed
> - Who can disclose it
> - Who can receive it
> - Purpose of disclosure
> - Expiration date/event
> - Signature + date
> 
> If any of these are missing → providers can refuse access.
> 
> ---
> 
> # What your Indiana document MUST explicitly say
> This is the practical checklist hospitals actually look for.
> 
> ## 1) Name the agent as “personal representative”
> Must say something like:
> 
> > My Health Care Representative is my **personal representative under HIPAA**.
> 
> This is the #1 most important sentence.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 2) Explicitly allow access EVEN WHEN patient has capacity
> This is huge. Many POAs only activate when incapacitated.
> 
> You want this wording:
> 
> > This authorization is effective immediately and continues during any period of incapacity.
> 
> Otherwise they can say: “Well she’s conscious so we can’t talk to you.”
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 3) Must cover ALL protected health information
> Needs language like:
> 
> > All medical and mental health records, including:
> - diagnosis
> - treatment
> - medications
> - lab results
> - billing
> - insurance
> - substance use records
> - HIV / AIDS
> - mental health records
> 
> Yes — mental health and substance use need explicit inclusion.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 4) Must allow communication with providers
> Include:
> 
> > May speak with and obtain information from any physician, hospital, pharmacy, insurer, or health provider.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 5) Must allow copies of records
> Include verbs like:
> 
> - inspect  
> - obtain  
> - receive  
> - copy  
> - release  
> 
> Hospitals love verbs.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 6) Must allow re-disclosure
> This prevents another roadblock.
> 
> Example:
> 
> > May disclose this information to others involved in care.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 7) Must have an expiration clause
> HIPAA requires an expiration event.
> 
> Typical wording:
> 
> > This authorization expires upon my death or written revocation.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 8) Must be signed + notarized/witnessed
> Indiana advance directives must be signed and:
> - witnessed by two adults **or**
> - notarized citeturn0search20
> 
> Most providers prefer notarized.
> 
> ---
> 
> # The “bulletproof clause” hospitals love

> 
> That single paragraph solves 90% of hospital pushback.
