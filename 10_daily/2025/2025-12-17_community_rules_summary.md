> ## Why This Is Weaker Than It Looks
> 
> ### 1. “Guest” ≠ “Occupant” (Legally)
> Courts do **not** treat guests as occupants unless there is evidence of:
> - Permanent residence
> - Receiving mail
> - Keeping belongings
> - Paying utilities or rent
> - Listing address elsewhere
> 
> Holiday family members:
> - Visiting
> - Sleeping temporarily
> - Eating meals
> - Leaving after holidays
> 
> → **Not occupants.**
> 
> Labeling them as such without proof is overreach.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 2. Family Visits Trigger Fair Housing Sensitivity
> Especially when:
> - The resident is elderly
> - Disabled
> - Receives care
> - Needs family support
> 
> Blanket restrictions on family visits can:
> - Violate Fair Housing Act protections
> - Be discriminatory if enforced selectively
> - Be unlawful if they interfere with caregiving or familial association
> 
> **“Consent will not be unreasonably withheld”** is a legal trap for the landlord — not the tenant.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 3. Holiday Context Matters
> Courts recognize:
> - Holidays = **predictable, temporary family gatherings**
> - Thanksgiving / Christmas / funerals / emergencies are **reasonable exceptions**
> - Rigid enforcement during holidays looks **punitive**, not protective
> 
> A landlord enforcing a strict 3-day rule during Christmas for a mother’s children or grandchildren is **inviting a fair-housing complaint**.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 4. The 21-Day Annual Cap Is Practically Unenforceable
> To enforce it, management would need:
> - Accurate tracking
> - Prior notice
> - Equal application to all tenants
> - Documentation over time
> 
> In practice:
> - Selective enforcement = discrimination
> - Inconsistent enforcement = invalid
> - Retroactive enforcement = unlawful
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 5. Burden of Proof Is on the Owner
> If they issue an “unauthorized occupant” notice, **they must prove**:
> - Duration exceeded limits
> - No consent was requested or granted
> - The person actually *resided* there
> - Harm or lease impact occurred
> 
> Most property managers cannot meet that burden.
>
