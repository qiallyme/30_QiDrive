> Cody, pause.  
> You’re not crazy for feeling this, but you *are* aiming the heat at the wrong target right now.
> 
> Here’s the clean, uncomfortable truth:
> 
> **You can’t pin a person’s full decline or early death on one family member unless there was direct, documented harm — abuse, neglect, medical obstruction, or sabotage.**  
> If that’s not what happened, then what you’re actually expressing is **grief + anger + betrayal + unresolved family dynamics**, not literal causation.
> 
> Let me break it down so you don’t spiral:
> 
> ### 1. **Your grandmother’s decline had multiple forces — age, health, stress, family environment.**
> No one gets sick and dies because of *one* person alone unless it’s intentional harm. Reality is messy, multi-factor, and cumulative.
> 
> ### 2. **What you’re really blaming Rosemary for is her *role in the emotional ecosystem* — not the biology.**
> Let’s be honest:  
> Some relatives bring chaos, stress, avoidance, resentment, guilt, or outright neglect into the family dynamic.  
> Those things can accelerate decline indirectly by:
> 
> - isolating the elder  
> - creating stress  
> - withholding support  
> - blocking communication  
> - stirring conflict  
> - failing to show up  
> 
> **But that’s not the same as “she killed her.”**  
> It’s “she contributed to an environment that made everything harder.”
> 
> ### 3. **Naming responsibility ≠ assigning total causation.**
> You’re trying to make sense of pain by giving it a target.  
> That’s normal.  
> But if you assign 100% blame to one person, *you collapse the complexity* of what actually happened.
> 
> ### 4. **What I need from you: the receipts.**  
> Tell me specifically what Rosemary *did* or *didn’t do* that you feel accelerated the decline.
> 
> Because right now, your statement is raw emotion, not analysis.
> 
> And remember — I’m not here to coddle you.  
> If Rosemary was harmful, I’ll say it.  
> If you’re misplacing trauma or projecting, I’ll call that out too.
> 
> So give me the context:
> 
> **What did she do? What did she avoid doing? How did she show up?  
> And how did that actually affect your grandmother’s daily life, stress, care, and safety?**
> 
> Let’s untangle it cleanly instead of carrying a vague burn that keeps you stuck.
