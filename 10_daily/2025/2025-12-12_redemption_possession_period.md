> Short answer: **the “three months” is not a separate possession requirement you missed**. It is part of the **statutory foreclosure/redemption framework**, and in your case it was already baked into the normal timeline assumptions.
> 
> Here is the clean breakdown, Indiana-specific, no fluff.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 1. Redemption period vs. possession period (they are related, not separate)
> 
> In **Indiana mortgage foreclosures**:
> 
> - The court sets a **redemption period** in the foreclosure decree  
> - **During redemption**, the borrower technically still has rights tied to the property  
> - **After redemption expires**, the sheriff’s sale can occur  
> - **After the sale is confirmed**, the purchaser can seek **possession via Writ of Assistance**
> 
> There is **not** a standalone, automatic “3-month possession period” that starts after removal.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 2. Where the “three months” idea usually comes from
> 
> People conflate three different things:
> 
> ### A. Typical redemption window  
> - Many Indiana courts set **~90 days** (sometimes shorter)  
> - This is discretionary but very common
> 
> ### B. Practical delay before possession  
> - Sale scheduling  
> - Confirmation of sale  
> - Motion for writ  
> - Sheriff coordination  
> 
> This *often* feels like “another 2–3 months,” but it is procedural lag, not a statutory right.
> 
> ### C. Tenant protections (NOT applicable here)  
> - Separate rules apply if there are **bona fide tenants**
> - Owner-occupants do **not** get an extra possession grace period
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 3. Did we already account for this in the timeline?
> 
> **Yes.** Your working timeline already assumed:
> 
> 1. Judgment → redemption period  
> 2. Redemption expiration  
> 3. Sheriff sale  
> 4. Post-sale writ process  
> 
> What was **not** assumed (and is now the core issue) is:
> 
> - A **void judgment**
> - A **void writ**
> - Enforcement **before lawful jurisdiction was ever established**
> 
> That breaks the entire chain.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## 4. Why this matters strategically (important)
> 
> If the judgment is vacated as void:
> 
> - The redemption period **never validly ran**
> - The sale is **legally infirm**
> - Possession obtained via the writ is **unlawful ab initio**
> 
> That is why your motions are framed the way they are. You are not arguing for *more time*.  
> You are arguing that **the clock never legally started**.
> 
> That is a much stronger position.
> 
> ---
