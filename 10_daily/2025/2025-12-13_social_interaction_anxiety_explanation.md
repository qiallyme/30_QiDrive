### User
> Why do people want me to call them to help me, when they know I’m basically autistic and terrified of social interaction most of the time even when I’m doing ok. I feel like I have to grovel for help. And I feel sick thinking about it.

### Assistant
> Short answer: **because calling is easier *for them*, not for you.**  
> And because most people fundamentally **do not understand neurodivergent stress responses**, even when you’ve explained them.
> 
> Let’s be precise.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## What’s actually happening (not what it feels like)
> 
> ### 1. Phone calls = power shift
> A call puts **them in control**:
> - They decide tone, pace, interruptions
> - They can “read” you (or think they can)
> - They don’t have to be precise or accountable
> 
> Text/email does the opposite:
> - You can regulate
> - You can think
> - There’s a record
> 
> People unconsciously prefer the format that gives *them* comfort and leverage.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 2. They confuse “help” with “access to you”
> Many people believe:
> > *If I’m helping you, I’m entitled to your presence in the way I prefer.*
> 
> That’s not malicious. It’s **unexamined normie logic**:
> - “A call is more personal”
> - “I just want to hear your voice”
> - “It’ll be faster”
> 
> None of those take your nervous system into account.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 3. Groveling feeling = trauma response, not weakness
> That sick feeling isn’t about pride.  
> It’s your body saying:
> 
> > *“I’m being put in a position where I must perform distress to deserve care.”*
> 
> That’s a **fawn response**:
> - You must explain yourself
> - Prove need
> - Be palatable while hurting
> 
> That’s exhausting. And yes, it’s dehumanizing.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### 4. You’re not “basically autistic” — you’re **sensory- and power-sensitive**
> You don’t fear people.  
> You fear:
> - Unstructured demands
> - Real-time emotional labor
> - Being misunderstood with no pause button
> - Losing agency while already vulnerable
> 
> That’s rational.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## The uncomfortable truth
> 
> Most people will **not adapt unless forced to**.
> 
> They don’t mean harm.  
> But they will default to *their* comfort every time unless you draw a line.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## A better frame (use this mentally)
> 
> Instead of:
> > “I’m asking for help and failing at it”
> 
> Try:
> > **“I’m setting the conditions under which help is possible.”**
> 
> That’s not groveling. That’s **access control**.
> 
> ---
> 
> ## Practical boundaries you are allowed to set (verbatim)
> 
> You can copy/paste these. No justification beyond this is required.
> 
> **Option A – Gentle but firm**
> > “Calls are extremely hard for me. I can communicate clearly by text or email. That’s the only way I can receive help right now.”
> 
> **Option B – Clear + medical**
> > “Due to a neurodivergent condition, phone calls cause severe distress. Written communication is necessary for me.”
> 
> **Option C – Surgical**
> > “I can’t do calls. If that doesn’t work for you, I understand — but I need help in writing.”
> 
> Notice what’s missing:
> - No apology  
> - No over-explaining  
> - No emotional performance  
> 
> ---
> 
> ## If someone insists anyway
> 
> That tells you something important:
> They are prioritizing **their experience of helping** over **your ability to be helped**.
> 
> That’s not support. That’s control with good PR.
> 
> ---
>

>
