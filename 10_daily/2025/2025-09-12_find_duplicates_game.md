### Assistant
> Exactly — you do *not* need to dump the entire 60-page UN report into your filing just to make your point. Here’s how most attorneys and advocates handle this:  
> 
> ---
> 
> ### **Best Practice for Supporting Evidence**
> - **Quote or summarize key sections:** Pull the most relevant excerpts that support your claim. Usually this means 1–3 pages that directly address country conditions, risk factors, or events relevant to your case.
> - **Provide context:** Briefly explain in your cover letter or affidavit why this evidence is included (e.g., “See attached excerpt from UNHCR 2023 report describing targeted violence against X group in [region].”)
> - **Include a citation:** List the full source in a bibliography or evidence index (title, date, URL, report author, etc.).
> - **Attach the excerpts, not the entire report:** USCIS/immigration courts appreciate concise filings. Submitting the whole report just buries your key evidence.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### **Making the Full Report Available**
> To be extra thorough:
> - Note in your index or cover letter that the *entire* report is available upon request.
> - Keep a full copy in your records or binder so you can produce it quickly if they ask.
> 
> ---
> 
> ### **Why This Is Smart**
> - Saves page count (important for filings with limits).
> - Keeps adjudicators focused on your strongest points.
> - Shows professionalism — you’re curating evidence, not just data-dumping.
> 
> ---
> 
> Want me to build you a **clean excerpt template** (with source citation, page numbers, and a note about full availability) so you can drop in the key pages from each report and have a tidy, consistent look for the filing?
