# 06 — Revocable Living Trust

## Purpose
A revocable living trust can be used to hold assets during life, provide management during incapacity, and direct distribution at death. It may help reduce probate exposure for assets properly transferred into the trust.

## Important Reality Check
A trust does not avoid probate by magic. Assets usually must be retitled or otherwise properly assigned to the trust for the trust to control them.

## Who May Want This
A revocable living trust may be useful for people who:
- own real estate in more than one state;
- want centralized management during incapacity;
- want privacy not available in probate;
- want more detailed distribution control; or
- want succession planning for certain assets.

## Who May Not Need This
A trust may be unnecessary or lower-priority for someone whose situation is simple and whose main assets already pass by beneficiary designation, joint ownership, or transfer-on-death structure.

# Part IV — After-Death Instructions

# 01 — Last Will and Testament

## What a Will Does
A last will and testament allows the client to:
- name an executor or personal representative;
- direct distribution of probate assets;
- nominate guardians for minor children;
- make specific gifts; and
- state final instructions relating to estate administration.

## What a Will Does Not Do
A will does not automatically control assets that pass by:
- beneficiary designation;
- joint ownership with survivorship rights;
- transfer-on-death or payable-on-death designation; or
- properly funded trust ownership.

Before completing the template below, the client should treat this section as a drafting form for review and proper execution, not as a finalized state-specific legal instrument.

## Last Will and Testament Template

# Part V — Execution, Storage & Reference

# 10 — Reference & Resource Section

## Plain-English Glossary
**Executor / Personal Representative:** the person who handles the estate after death.  
**Agent / Attorney-in-Fact:** the person named under a power of attorney.  
**Health-Care Agent / Proxy:** the person authorized to make medical decisions if the client cannot.  
**Trustee:** the person or institution that manages a trust.  
**Fiduciary:** a person who has a legal duty to act in another person’s best interest.  
**Beneficiary:** a person or organization receiving assets or benefits.  
**Intestate:** dying without a valid will, which leaves distribution to state law.  
**Probate:** the legal process for administering certain property after death.  
**Living Will:** a document stating treatment preferences in serious medical circumstances.  
**Durable Power of Attorney:** a document allowing financial and legal action by an agent despite incapacity.  
**Revocable Trust:** a trust that may be changed or revoked during life.

## What Each Document in This Package Does
- **Will:** directs probate distribution and names an executor.
- **Living Will:** states end-of-life treatment preferences.
- **Durable Power of Attorney:** authorizes financial and legal decision-making.
- **HIPAA Authorization:** permits access to medical information.
- **Medical Power of Attorney:** names a health-care decision-maker.
- **Revocable Living Trust:** may centralize asset management and reduce probate exposure if funded.
- **Beneficiary Designation Worksheet:** aligns account-level inheritance choices.
- **Digital Estate Directive:** organizes digital life and access planning.
- **Final Wishes Addendum:** records personal preferences and messages.

## Questions to Ask an Attorney
- Does my state require a specific statutory form for any of these documents?
- Do witness and notary requirements differ by document?
- Would a trust meaningfully benefit my situation?
- How should I coordinate my home, vehicles, and bank accounts with my plan?
- Are my minor children or dependent adults adequately protected?
- Do I need a pour-over will, a transfer-on-death deed, or special beneficiary planning?
- Do I need tax-sensitive or disability-sensitive planning?

## When to Update Your Estate Plan
Update after:
- marriage, divorce, or remarriage;
- birth, adoption, or guardianship changes;
- death or incapacity of a named fiduciary;
- a major asset purchase or sale;
- moving to a new state; or
- major health changes.

## Supporting Records to Gather
- deeds and property records;
- account statements;
- insurance policies;
- retirement plan summaries;
- beneficiary forms;
- marriage, divorce, and adoption records;
- business ownership documents;
- loan information;
- a list of debts; and
- a digital account inventory.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### Do I need a trust to have an estate plan?
No. Many people still benefit from a will, powers of attorney, health-care directives, and beneficiary coordination even without a trust.

### Does a will control everything I own?
No. Some assets pass outside the will based on title or beneficiary designation.

### Is a power of attorney still valid after death?
Generally, no. Authority under a power of attorney typically ends at death.

### If I already named beneficiaries, do I still need a will?
Usually, yes. A will can address guardianship, the appointment of a personal representative, residual assets, and gaps not covered by beneficiary designations.
